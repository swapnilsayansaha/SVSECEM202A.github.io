Related Work

Activity Recognition Using Earable Sensors:
Hossain et al. [1] proposed a device-gateway-cloud (DGC) framework (with edge computing capabilities) for inferencing 6 activity modes from earable inertial data pertinent to head-mouth mechanics, namely “speak”, “eat”, “head shake”, “head nod”, “stay” and “walk”, obtaining classification accuracies of 81.20% and 88.33% using K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) and Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) on 50467 frames (90 seconds per frame) via a windowing approach through the application of handcrafted time-domain statistical features (KNN) and automatic feature extraction (CNN). Prakash et al. [2] exploited the natural filtering of noise/vibrations (associated with inertial sensor data) as inertial signals propagate from lower-body to the ear canals to develop a subject and pattern-independent step counting reflex model (called STEAR) using dynamic time warp (DTW) algorithm, achieving an average step-counting accuracy of 95% across five activity modes, as well as devising a method for accurate calculation of jumping heights. Whilst natural filtering of inertial signals might pose a barrier for detection of fine-grained activity modes, such as gym activities, Radhakrishnan et al. [3] showed that fusing earable inertial data and equipment inertial data via application of time-frequency domain correlation mechanisms can yield 92% classification accuracy of 8 exercise modes (using random forests) on 144 exercise frames, with the earable and equipment signals correctly pairing with each other 83% of the time in a multi-user environment. Atallah et al [4] validated the use of a 3DOF earable sensor for gait monitoring by correlating the vertical, medio-lateral and anterior-posterior acceleration features with cadence, impulse peak-forces, length of stride, peak applied force and weight acceptance rate from a piezoelectric force-plate instrumented treadmill, obtaining an average R^2 of 0.355. Nirjon et al. [5] illustrated the fusion of microphone and 3DOF inertial data from an earable device for heart rate and activity detection, with the data to be used for an adaptive music recommendation system. They achieved an activity classification accuracy of 96.8% on 3 levels (low, medium, high) of 7 activity modes on data from 17 subjects. Gil et al. [6] fused ECG, impedance, amperometic and potentiometric measurements with 3 DOF inertial measurements (all within a single earable sensor) in order to monitor sweat and cardiovascular parameters during physical exercise, with acceleration data complementing physiological measurements. ​Hölzemann et al. [7] presented an activity data annotation method to synchronize inertial measurements in a multi-sensor environment via button presses on the earable sensors as markers using 375967 frames (@50Hz) and 2255764 frames (@300 Hz) of 6 activity modes (namely “read/desk work”, “walk”, “climbing stairs”, “sit”, “dribble a basketball” and “rest”) from earable and wrist-mounted wearable sensors respectively, achieving a median time mismatch of 1.1 seconds. 
Head-Pose Estimation Using Inertial Sensors:
With the advent of virtual reality (VR) applications, the innate challenges of head tracking using inertial sensors have been explored significantly in literature. Foxlin [8] developed a sensor-fusion (accelerometer and gyroscope) algorithm based on separate-bias complementary Kalman filters in order to counteract temporal drift and reduce latency. Ferlini et al. [9] proposed the fusion of 6DOF inertial data from two earable sensors in order to estimate final head position using quaternions, delta motions and complementary filters, achieving estimation errors as low as 5.4 degrees and 18.7 degrees for short and long movements (noisier) respectively. They also experimentally demonstrated the challenges of integrating a magnetometer (useful for tracking absolute and incremental motions) inside earbuds, which is prone to interference from the speakers. The fusion algorithm was based on the gyroscopic integration, tilt/yaw correction and predictive/positional tracking methods intended to reduce latency and support accurate prediction with drift correction for the Oculus Rift [10]. Odoemelem et al. [11] estimated the Euler angles of the head using earable inertial data in order to control the pitch and yaw of a robot arm with an accuracy of 9mm from the target center. Lin et al. [12] fused 6DOF inertial data from the Oculus Rift and VR controllers in order to detect full body and head poses via a recurrent neural network (RNN) and developed a Unity integration package supporting inertial data-driven pose estimation, achieving a maximum mean absolute error of 10.76 cm for different joints and maximum average distance error of 3.13 cm. Anthes et al. [13] provided examples of several state-of-the-art commercial inertial head pose tracking systems for VR as follows:
  • Input devices: STEM and Perception Neuron
  • Output devices:
    • Mobile head mounted displays (HMD): Gameface Mark, Samsung GearVR, Google Cardboard, Zeiss VR One and Auravisor
    • Stationary/Wired HMD: Oculus Rift/DK, PlayStation VR, HTC Vive and Avegant Glyph, FOVE, HDK OSVR, AntVR, Sulon Cortex and VRvana Totem
    • Multisensory displays: Nirvana Mask
They also stated several inertial tracking algorithms commonly used in VR head tracking systems such as time warp (synchronous and asynchronous), predictive tracking and occasional visual tracking to mitigate temporal drift. They mentioned that the latency should not be greater than 20 ms in such systems.
Implementing Spatial Audio Dynamics:

​Humans detect a sound source by interaural information. By reproducing sounds in left and right ears with disparities in, made up of ITD (Interaural Time Difference) and ILD (Interaural Level Difference), location of the sound source can be reproduced horizontally [14]. To evaluate the forward-backward difference and elevation difference, monaural spectral filtering is used [15]. Analyzing spectrum pattern can also help in perceiving sound sources vertically and determining front or back sources. Apart from ITD, ILD and spectral cues, other cues include cues from torso diffraction and reflection, direct to reverberant energy ratio, familiarity with sound source and cue augmentation due to head motion [14].
 
In order to implement the auditory cues, head-related transfer function (HRTF) is applied. HRTF is a transfer function that models the sound pressure on human ears at a certain relative position with respect to the sound source as output and the original sound pressure at the same position without human as input, which describes acoustic path from sound source to ear canals and reflects the interference of human body shadow effect and orientation change. By modulating the HRTFs, we can play back the disparities of human ears and reproduce spatial audio [16]. Examples of HRTF datasets include the CIPIC HRTF dataset [17] and the ARI HRTF dataset [18]. An innate issue with HRTF is that HRTFs are highly dependent on individual features. A HRTF rendered from a person might lead to unacceptable error when applying it to another person [19]. Therefore, it is essential to optimize the individualization of HRTFs efficiently and accurately. Xu et al. [19] outlined several classical methods to individualize HRTFs:
 
  • Direct measurement (most accurate but most time-consuming and non-practical). A non-classical approach for direct measurement is fast individualization measurement (static head pose [20] or dynamic head pose [21])
  • Partial individualization by averaging HRTFs or using typical HRTFs.
  • Individualization by subjective selection, e.g. optimizing HRTF databases via perceptual selection [22].
  • Individualization by scaling/grouping non-individual HRTFs, e.g. using existing HRTF datasets to find the best fit [23] (also called anthropomorphic matching).
  • Individualization by theoretical computation, e.g. finite element simulations [24] based on individualized anthropometry.
  • Indirect individualization using physical features
  • Individualization via tuning, i.e. calibrate the subjective criteria of non-individualized HRTF.
 
Commonly, three techniques are used for spatial audio reproduction, namely channel-based (MPEG Surround, SASC), object-based (SAOC) and transform-domain-based technique (DirAC) [16], with the channel-based technique being the most dominant in the market and having the pros of being easy to setup and no pre-processing needs with the ability to generate stereo and multichannel stereo surround sound. However, object-based techniques offer accurate sound image and provide fine-grained reproduction attributes such as amplitude panning and transaural rendering.
 
Audio spatializer plug-ins (not individualized) are commercially available for use in VR platforms, such as Oculus and Microsoft HRTF spatializer in Unity [25]. Zijlstra [26] exploited the audio spatialization capabilities of the Microsoft HoloLens MR platform and built an indoor navigation system for the visually impaired. Murphy et al. [27] outlined several spatialization APIs used in computer games and VR environments such as Java 3D (no support for HRTF datasets), Microsoft XNA/XACT Surround and MPEG-4 2.0 for static computer systems and JSR-234/AMMS and OpenSL ES for mobile platforms.
Fusing Head Inertial Data with Binaural Audio:
The first step to create a binaural audio from a monoaural sound involves applying an FIR filter based on HRTF cues. Although HRTF automatically encodes elevation and azimuth information of the head [28], real time head pose estimation is required in order to interpolate the HRTF and get the right combination of the FIR filter for given relative position set. In other words, the HRTF filters are made essentially for a static sound source whose relative position to the head is fixed and is not usable for VR applications directly as the relative position of sound source is now dependent on head orientation. Serafin et al. [29] mentioned that head-tracking components that are embedded in VR headsets have low drift and latency, ensuring performance less than the critical latencies in order to exploit the HRTF spatial connection. Zotkin et al. [30] proposed a complete audio rendering system for virtual reality environments. They use a low latency Polhemus tracker mounted on the headphones to get the position and the orientation of the sound source. The sound source coordinates are then found with respect to the relative frame of headphones and using this angle of elevation and azimuth the filter parameters from HRTF are found. Algazi et al. [14] proposed a motion-tracked binaural (MTB) system for use with microphone arrays. MTB uses a simple signal interpolation to generate the spatial audio using head tracking. Such a system may be implemented in a multi-user MR system (e.g. CONIX ARENA) where multiple people may interact with virtual objects. 

In Oculus SDK, spatialization and head tracking packages convert a monophonic source into a sound source emanating from a particular object at a particular position. If the orientation is known, the SDK lets the developer to project sound towards listener’s space irrespective of head position [31]. The Google VR API provides sound object rendering capabilities which creates a virtual sound source in 3D and spatializes it based on the current viewing angle (gaze) [32]. The spatial workstation and 360 sound kit by Facebook has two head related configurations. In the first mode (known as “head-locked”), an object’s relative position with respect to the head is fixed and this ensures that the sound moves as the head moves. In the second configuration, spatialized mono audio can be converted stereo spatialized sound [33].  Unity’s audio spatializer plugins can be used in specialized VR headsets, extracting inertial information during use and regulating the gain of left and right ear differently to create the effect of binaural audio. Most of the commercial plugins have the gain controls embedded in the video and uses the headset’s sensors to determine the orientation of the head and thus regulate the sound accordingly [34]. Samsung VR provides an option to use sound recordings to play spatial sound. For instance, it can spatialize even mono audio and a 4-channel binaural audio by playing a combination of exactly two different channels based on head position obtained [35].
